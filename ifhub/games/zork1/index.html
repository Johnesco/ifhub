Zork I - The Great Underground Empire

Inform 7 Edition

Play Latest Version (with sound)

I’ve played video games for a long time. Video? I’ve played games with NO graphics. As a kid I stared into a CRT monitor hooked to an Atari 800 computer and marveled at the description: “You are standing in an open field west of a white house, with a boarded front door.” I’d type in short phrases — pure text. Type “open mailbox” and the game would do it — all in text. I saw so many advancements from that point. Sounds, graphics, 2D and 3D. From a few pixels on the screen to ultra-real worlds. And yet, I often still thought of that white house.

The game in question is Zork — a game of adventure, danger, and low cunning — and it’s now open source. These games, in a genre sometimes called “Text-Adventures” and sometimes “Interactive Fiction” (or IF), just gave you a text prompt (hey there’s a switch) and you responded.

Among so many computer advances, along came AI. Love it or hate it, I had to try it, and set out to see if I could code beyond my current scope without making a mess (well a mess, sure, but that compiles). I figured, “it’s just text,” and set Claude Code to translate from the ZIL source to Inform 7 syntax (an engine descended from Infocom architecture). The result was much more readable. And BOOM! It did it in one go and it compiled! Huzzah! But… it’s missing something. Why did I notice something was missing? I’m a tester at heart and always have a keen eye for little changes.

I got into a dialogue with Claude, something like, “hey, why is this detail in ZIL but not in the translation?” And so began a long introduction into the ZIL code format. There are shortcuts being taken here for sure, but using AI will force you to realize you’ve got to be in the driver’s seat, at least for now. We began to look at all text, piece by piece, every bit, discovering together the odd format of the old engine, bound by such tight limitations, and the loose translation we began to refine.

Using ZILF, we compiled the ZIL to the .z5 format and ran it online. We had a working version of the original, warts and all. Claude began to suggest trying to test outcomes deterministically. I reminded Claude about the random aspects of Zork. Claude mentioned we could compile with a specific seed to mitigate randomness and produce expected results (as it compared transcripts). Transcripts. I mean — it is text after all. Every playthrough, we stored the transcripts. We’d compare transcripts of the original and the translation side by side. That developed into a testing system that I’m now expanding to test the games.

This wasn’t a one-shot vibe code. It was a deep dive into AI and IF.

Below are snapshots of each milestone. v0 is the purest version of the original Zork I commercial release. v1 attempts to translate it into Inform 7 — if it’s in the ZIL code, it’s in v1 (any bugs naturally fixed by the move to a modern engine will remain fixed, we won’t force bugs to remain). v2 is where real improvement begins — only here do I allow myself to actually fix anything that was ever a bug in the original ZIL. The latest version builds on all of that — now with ambient audio and sound effects — but it’s really v0 to v1 being given huge attention.

Version History

v4 — Modern IF (Current)

Applying modern interactive fiction best practices for fluid, fun gameplay.

  • Richer parser responses and smoother player interactions
  • Better default messages and more helpful feedback
  • Native Glk sound: audio bundled in .gblorb via Parchment’s Emglken WASM engine — ambient zones and sound effects driven directly from story.ni
  • Inherits all v3 audio design, v2 bug fixes, and v1 content
Play (with sound) Source Walkthrough

v3 — Sound

Intentionally diverging from the original, leaning into what Inform 7 does best.

  • Ambient audio: zone-based background music across 10 zones (forest, cave, house, water, rapids, loud, hades, mine, machinery, silence) with smooth crossfades between areas
  • Sound effects: 16 one-shot audio triggers for key game events — puzzle moments, creature encounters, and atmospheric touches. Audio files are CC0 placeholders, not final
  • Native Glk sound: audio is now driven from story.ni via Glk sound channels, bundled as a .gblorb and played through Parchment’s Emglken WASM engine
  • Inherits all v2 bug fixes and v1 content

v2 — Bug Fixes

The first version that changes the game rather than just translating it. This is also where the testing methodology was built — deterministic seed-based walkthroughs, side-by-side ZIL vs. Inform 7 transcript diffing, and automated regression testing.

  • Fix: Deflating the boat lost its contents — a “Raft of Holding” bug (#2)
  • Fix: Putting the raft in the coffin and the coffin in the raft caused both to vanish (#3)
  • Fix: Phantom combat — you could attack enemies after they left the room (#6)
  • Fix: Rising water killed the player even inside the boat (#15)
  • Fix: Grate could be locked while open (#16)
  • Fix: Sharp objects puncturing the boat also destroyed its contents (#22)
  • Fix: Lit match survived death and resurrection (#11)
  • Fix: Weapon penalty applied even on combat misses (#4)
  • Translation fixes: 12 behavioral differences found via transcript comparison (#63)
Play Source Walkthrough

v1 — The Port

A complete, playable, and winnable ZIL-to-Inform 7 translation — 110 rooms, 138 objects, every puzzle faithfully reproduced. The goal was a 1:1 gameplay match with the original.

  • 110 rooms across five regions, all 19 treasures, 350-point scoring system with ranked titles
  • NPCs: roaming thief (5-turn patrol cycle, 75% steal rate, treasure redistribution), troll combat with unconscious/recovery mechanic, cyclops with multiple solutions
  • Full puzzle systems: dam and reservoir, exorcism ceremony, coal-to-diamond machine, inflatable boat and river, mirror rooms
  • 377 missing text responses restored from ZIL source in a 6-phase audit (#44)
  • Dead player ghost state — explore as spirit with dimly-lit rooms and prayer resurrection
  • Sword glow scans adjacent rooms and detects all three villains, matching ZIL logic

v0 — Original ZIL

The unmodified Infocom ZIL source code, exactly as released by Activision under the MIT License.

  • 10 ZIL source files (~12,000 lines) with navigable definitions
  • Annotation toggle explaining game engine and puzzle mechanics
  • Playable game compiled from the original ZIL using ZILF, the open-source ZIL compiler, producing a Z-machine story file (.z3)
  • Runs in-browser via Parchment, a JavaScript Z-machine interpreter — no downloads required
